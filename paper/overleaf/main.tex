% -------------------- Abstract --------------------
\begin{abstract}
Edge--cloud visual simultaneous localization and mapping (VSLAM) can exploit cloud-side computation to perform more complex perception, optimization, and mapping tasks. However, it also requires transmitting dense visual streams from resource-constrained edge devices to the cloud, which easily creates a bandwidth bottleneck and introduces significant upload latency and communication overhead under constrained links. Conventional image and video codecs mainly optimize pixel-level or perceptual fidelity, and their objectives are not explicitly designed around the geometric continuity and trackable structures required by VSLAM. Inspired by sparse key scenes and high-level semantic cues in human visual memory, this paper proposes VLMem-Codec, a vision--language memory coding framework for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM. The edge encoder converts a dense visual sequence into VLMem, or Vision--Language Memory, and transmits it as a compact VLMem payload. Each VLMem payload unit consists of visual anchors and language cues: the visual anchors constrain endpoint appearance and geometry, while the language cues provide compact motion, semantic, and reconstruction conditions. On the cloud side, a world-model decoder reconstructs omitted frames from the transmitted VLMem conditions. Meanwhile, we introduce Memory-Guided Decoder Refinement (MGDR), implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments, to bias the decoder toward the visual continuity, geometric consistency, and trackable structures required by VSLAM without retraining the whole world model. The proposed coding framework is evaluated using downstream VSLAM absolute pose error (APE) RMSE, transmission reduction, edge-side motion-estimation cost, and auxiliary image-fidelity metrics. Experiments show an average 96.8\% transmission reduction, reconstructed-sequence APE close to original-sequence APE on average, low-cost phase-correlation motion cues, and a reduction in reconstructed-sequence APE with MGDR in the decoder ablation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ==================================================

Visual simultaneous localization and mapping (VSLAM) enables mobile robots, autonomous platforms, and embodied intelligent systems to estimate their motion and build environment maps from visual observations. With the increasing availability of cloud-side computation, Edge--Cloud VSLAM has become an attractive system paradigm: the edge device captures visual observations under limited onboard resources, while the cloud undertakes more complex perception, optimization, and mapping tasks. However, this division also shifts a critical burden to the communication link. Edge-side visual sensors continuously produce high-frame-rate image streams, and transmitting dense visual data from the edge to the cloud introduces substantial bandwidth occupation, energy cost, and upload latency. This communication bottleneck becomes more severe in long-term navigation, large-scale deployment, and bandwidth-constrained wireless environments.

% Suggested Fig. 1: motivation and bandwidth-bottleneck concept figure.
\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig1.png}
\caption{Motivation of VLMem-Codec for Edge--Cloud VSLAM. Dense visual streaming creates a bandwidth bottleneck, whereas VLMem transmits key scenes as visual anchors together with compact language cues for cloud-side world-model reconstruction.}
\label{fig:concept}
\end{figure}

Existing image and video compression standards mainly reduce transmission load by exploiting spatial and temporal pixel redundancy. Such codecs are highly effective for human viewing, and their rate--distortion objectives are typically designed around visual fidelity, perceptual quality, and compact delivery of visually faithful frames. However, these objectives are not explicitly designed for the requirements of VSLAM. A compressed stream may spend a large number of bits preserving fine appearance details, color variations, or human-perceptual textures, while such information does not necessarily provide stable trackable structures for camera pose estimation.

This objective mismatch becomes more prominent when higher compression ratios are pursued. Distortions such as blur, blocking artifacts, texture loss, or temporal inconsistency may weaken feature repeatability, disturb trackable structures, and reduce the geometric cues used by downstream pose estimation. Conversely, VSLAM does not require pixel-identical or high-definition reconstruction of every frame; it requires sufficient geometric consistency, feature repeatability, trackable structures, and scene-motion continuity. Therefore, Edge--Cloud VSLAM transmission should not be treated merely as conventional video delivery, but as a task-oriented and communication-constrained visual transmission problem. In this problem, the transmitted representation should be constructed and evaluated around downstream VSLAM trajectory quality. Fig.~\ref{fig:concept} conceptually illustrates this bandwidth bottleneck and the corresponding memory-based transmission motivation, while the quantitative compression and trajectory tradeoffs are reported in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

As an engineering analogy, this paper is inspired by human visual memory. When navigating through an environment, humans do not need to remember every pixel of every frame; they often retain sparse key scenes, landmarks, motion transitions, and high-level semantic impressions. This intuition suggests a compact representation for Edge--Cloud VSLAM: a dense visual stream can be represented by VLMem, or Vision--Language Memory. In this representation, visual anchors at the boundaries of each VLMem unit preserve endpoint appearance and geometry, while language cues provide compact conditions about motion state, scene context, and reconstruction constraints. The cloud then uses world-model reconstruction to infer VSLAM-usable visual continuity across omitted intervals.

The key intuition is that many pixel-level details can be omitted when task-useful visual structures are preserved. VLMem-Codec aims to reduce the transmission of visual redundancy that is weakly related to VSLAM, while retaining visual structures useful for trajectory estimation through a VLMem payload composed of visual anchors and language cues. This differs from simple keyframe transmission because the omitted intervals are not ignored as gaps between adjacent transmitted frames; instead, they are reconstructed by a cloud-side world-model decoder under vision--language conditions.

Based on this formulation, we propose VLMem-Codec, a vision--language memory coding framework for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM. For each VLMem unit, the edge transmits visual anchors as geometric and appearance conditions and encodes language cues that describe motion state, scene priors, and reconstruction constraints. The visual anchors constrain the start and end views of the unit, while the language cues represent part of the omitted visual information with a compact abstraction. The cloud-side world-model decoder uses its learned visual prior to reconstruct task-usable intermediate frames, and the reconstructed sequence is then fed into a cloud-side VSLAM estimator for trajectory estimation.

The key mechanism of VLMem-Codec is the combination of three complementary information sources. First, visual anchors preserve endpoint visual and geometric conditions that are difficult to describe using language alone. Second, language cues encode high-level motion and semantic priors with low communication cost. Third, the cloud-side world-model decoder supplies plausible intermediate visual continuity from its learned prior under the VLMem conditions. In this way, dense visual transmission is reformulated as compact VLMem payload transmission rather than a generic video compression problem.

To make the framework practical for Edge--Cloud VSLAM, VLMem-Codec adopts a lightweight edge-side memory encoder. Instead of running expensive online image captioning or dense optical-flow networks on the edge, the encoder uses low-cost visual measurements such as phase correlation to generate coarse motion responses, and combines them with semantic responses to identify event structures and visual anchors. These responses are then converted into structured language cues to constrain cloud-side reconstruction. In the cloud decoding stage, Memory-Guided Decoder Refinement (MGDR) biases the decoder toward the visual continuity, geometric consistency, and trackable structures required by VSLAM. MGDR is implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments, so the pretrained world model can remain frozen while the decoder is adapted to VLMem conditions. In this work, reconstruction is evaluated primarily by downstream VSLAM trajectory quality using absolute pose error (APE) RMSE. Transmission reduction, edge-side motion-estimation cost, and auxiliary image-fidelity metrics are also reported to characterize the tradeoffs among communication efficiency, edge computation cost, and reconstruction quality.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose VLMem-Codec, a vision--language memory coding framework for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM, which replaces dense-frame transmission with a compact VLMem payload.
    \item We design a lightweight edge-side VLMem encoder that selects visual anchors, extracts coarse motion and semantic event cues, and converts them into compact language cues without relying on expensive online image captioning.
    \item We develop cloud-side world-model reconstruction, in which visual anchors and language cues jointly constrain task-usable intermediate-frame reconstruction from omitted visual intervals.
    \item We introduce MGDR, implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments, to perform task-oriented decoder refinement under VLMem conditions without retraining the whole world model, and analyze its effect using downstream trajectory accuracy and auxiliary image-fidelity metrics.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related_work} reviews related work on Edge--Cloud VSLAM transmission, sparse visual memory and vision--language cues for navigation, and world models for visual sequence reconstruction. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} presents the proposed VLMem-Codec framework. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports the experimental evaluation and ablation studies. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} presents the discussion and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
% ==================================================


\subsection{Edge--Cloud VSLAM}

Visual SLAM enables robots and autonomous platforms to estimate camera motion and build maps from continuous visual observations. Classical VSLAM systems mainly rely on local feature matching, photometric consistency, and nonlinear optimization to achieve tracking and mapping. Representative systems such as ORB-SLAM2, ORB-SLAM3, LSD-SLAM, DSO, and SVO have established mature geometric pipelines with good engineering efficiency~\cite{murartal2017orbslam2,campos2021orbslam3,engel2014lsdslam,engel2018dso,forster2014svo}. However, their robustness is often tied to stable textures, reliable features, and favorable imaging conditions. In scenes with weak texture, dynamic interference, fast motion, or severe illumination variation, feature repeatability and data association may degrade, leading to tracking drift or failure. Recent learning-based VSLAM methods, such as DROID-SLAM, improve robustness by introducing deep networks, multi-frame recurrent optimization, and dense correspondence modeling~\cite{teed2021droidslam}. These advantages, however, come with higher computation, memory, and power requirements, making direct deployment on lightweight edge platforms difficult.

Edge--cloud VSLAM has therefore emerged as a natural system paradigm for reconciling edge deployability with cloud-side computational capacity. Existing studies mainly reduce system burden from two perspectives. The first line focuses on collaborative computation, where tracking, local mapping, loop closure, or global optimization are distributed between the edge and the cloud through task partitioning, asynchronous collaboration, or submap-level processing~\cite{rabaud2011c2tam,kuffner2010cloud,shankar2020fogros,chen2024reviewcloudedge,chen2024cloudlearning,chen2024implicittransmission}. The second line optimizes transmitted representations, such as submaps, compact map structures, implicit representations, or cross-representation associations, to reduce the amount of map or intermediate data exchanged between edge and cloud systems~\cite{chen2024reviewcloudedge,chen2024implicittransmission,huo2025vcslam,chen2024crossdata,chen2025xrepslam}. These studies have advanced the system design of cloud-edge SLAM and VSLAM, but they mainly address how computation is allocated, how map results are represented, or how structured outputs are exchanged.

By contrast, the raw edge-to-cloud visual upload link remains one of the heaviest communication components. For cloud-side VSLAM, image sequences usually have to be uploaded before perception, optimization, and mapping can be performed. The data volume grows rapidly with image resolution, frame rate, number of cameras, and operating duration. In bandwidth-constrained environments, the cloud may have sufficient computation but still fail to exploit it if dense visual streams cannot be uploaded continuously. Thus, the communication problem in Edge--Cloud VSLAM eventually leads to a more fundamental question: how to compress and organize edge-side image sequences before cloud-side VSLAM, so that transmission load is substantially reduced while sufficient information remains for localization and mapping.

\subsection{VSLAM-Oriented Visual Compression}

Image and video compression provide the direct technical basis for reducing visual upload load. Conventional video coding standards exploit spatial and temporal redundancy through prediction, transform, quantization, and entropy coding, forming a mature ecosystem for general video delivery~\cite{sullivan2012hevc,sullivan2012h264,bross2021vvc}. Learned image and video compression further model image statistics through neural encoders, entropy models, hyperpriors, autoregressive priors, or attention mechanisms, improving visual quality at low bitrates~\cite{balle2018variational,minnen2018joint,agustsson2020scale,mentzer2020highfidelity}. These methods demonstrate that visual data contain substantial redundancy and can be transmitted compactly through coordinated encoding and decoding.

However, conventional compression objectives are mainly designed around pixel distortion, perceptual quality, or visual naturalness, rather than the geometric continuity and trackable structures required by VSLAM. On the one hand, a codec may spend bits preserving color variations, fine textures, or appearance details that improve visual fidelity but contribute little to pose estimation. This limits further compression from a VSLAM-task perspective. On the other hand, when stronger compression sacrifices image quality, blur, blocking artifacts, texture removal, or temporal inconsistency may damage local gradients, feature repeatability, and cross-frame geometric relations, thereby weakening VSLAM front-end tracking. Therefore, the limitation is not that existing codecs lack compression capability, but that their rate--distortion objectives are not fully aligned with the information needed by VSLAM.

This mismatch suggests that Edge--Cloud VSLAM does not require pixel-faithful transmission of every frame. Instead, it requires the preservation of information sufficient for pose estimation and mapping. Human navigation memory provides a useful analogy. When people navigate through an environment, they do not remember every pixel of the continuous visual stream; they tend to retain key scenes, landmarks, motion transitions, and distinctive semantic objects. Studies on event perception suggest that continuous experience is segmented into event units according to changes in prediction, attention, and memory~\cite{zacks2001event,zacks2007event}. Mental-model theory also indicates that internal representations often preserve structural relationships rather than exhaustive sensory details~\cite{johnsonlaird1983mental}.

This sparse navigation-memory view motivates a different way of thinking about visual compression for VSLAM: dense image sequences may be represented by a small number of key visual conditions and high-level semantic cues, rather than by all appearance details of all frames. The key question then becomes how to determine which visual information should be retained and which omitted content can be recovered from context and prior knowledge. Video event understanding, keyframe selection, and vision--language models provide useful technical foundations for this direction. Event boundary detection and change-point detection can identify important temporal changes in continuous videos~\cite{shou2021gebd,adams2007bayesian}, while vision--language models map visual content into compact semantic spaces and enable language-level descriptions of salient objects, scene context, and task-relevant content~\cite{radford2021clip,li2023blip2}. Nevertheless, these techniques alone do not directly solve how omitted visual intervals should be reconstructed on the cloud side. A reconstruction-capable decoding mechanism is still required.

\subsection{Generative Sequence Reconstruction}

Generative compression shifts compression from preserving all information to recovering omitted information from learned priors. Instead of requiring the bitstream to describe the original signal as completely as possible, generative compression uses GANs, diffusion models, or foundation generative models to recover visually plausible content at the decoder under very low bitrate conditions~\cite{mentzer2020highfidelity}. This indicates that the decoder can act not only as a passive bitstream reconstructor, but also as a prior-driven reconstruction module.

If a single image contains local distributional regularities that can be learned by generative models, adjacent frames in an image sequence also contain exploitable spatiotemporal regularities. Continuous frames are not independent images; they are constrained by camera motion, scene geometry, and semantic changes. Their predictability and reconstruction difficulty vary across straight motion, turning, viewpoint changes, scene transitions, object appearances, and object disappearances. Therefore, sequence compression should not only consider single-frame visual quality, but should also identify event structures and determine where stronger transmitted constraints are needed.

Video event segmentation, change-point detection, and streaming visual representation learning provide a basis for identifying such structures~\cite{shou2021gebd,adams2007bayesian,streamer2023}. These methods divide continuous visual input into event units with internal consistency and locate boundaries where motion state or scene content changes significantly. Within the same event unit, adjacent frames usually exhibit stronger motion and semantic continuity, while event boundaries require stronger visual or semantic constraints. Vision--language models further make it possible to encode semantic changes, salient objects, and scene context as compact cues. Thus, image-sequence compression can move beyond bitrate reduction alone and use motion events and semantic events to organize sparse but reconstructable conditions.

World models provide a further possibility for reconstructing visual continuity from such sparse conditions. Early world models learned latent dynamics of environment states, allowing agents to predict future states or perform imagination-based planning~\cite{ha2018worldmodels,hafner2019planet,hafner2020dreamer,hafner2023dreamerv3}. More recent predictive representation learning, transformer-based sequence models, diffusion models, and image-to-video or text-to-video generation models show that large learned models can synthesize temporally coherent visual sequences from sparse visual or language conditions~\cite{lecun2022path,assran2024vjepa,blattmann2023svd,ho2022videodiffusion,villegas2022phenaki}. In navigation and embodied AI, world models have also been used to imagine future observations, generate action-conditioned videos, and support planning~\cite{yang2024unisim,du2023learning,wu2023daydreamer,wang2025targetbench,bar2025navigation}. These models are not physically exact simulators and do not reproduce human cognition. Nevertheless, they provide an engineering approximation to the human ability to infer intermediate visual continuity from key scenes, motion experience, and semantic memory: large-scale spatiotemporal priors can help reconstruct omitted visual intervals under sparse conditions.

Using world models for Edge--Cloud VSLAM still requires caution. Generic video generation models usually emphasize visual realism, diversity, prompt adherence, or future prediction, whereas VSLAM depends more on stable viewpoints, geometric continuity, feature repeatability, and trajectory-level utility. Generated frames may contain local hallucinations, texture drift, geometric inconsistency, or dynamic-object artifacts. These artifacts may be acceptable for human viewing but harmful to downstream pose estimation. Therefore, world models should not be treated as generic video enhancement modules for VSLAM. They need to be combined with event segmentation, motion/semantic cues, and VSLAM-oriented evaluation, so that reconstruction is assessed by downstream trajectory quality rather than image fidelity alone.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Framework Overview and Problem Formulation}
\label{subsec:framework_formulation}

VLMem-Codec formulates image transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM as a communication-constrained and task-oriented visual transmission problem. Instead of directly uploading a dense image stream to the cloud, the edge first constructs a compact VLMem payload from the continuous visual sequence. The payload is centered on visual anchors and language cues: visual anchors preserve the appearance and geometric conditions at the two ends of each VLMem unit, while language cues describe motion states, semantic changes, and reconstruction constraints within the omitted interval. The cloud then uses a world-model decoder to reconstruct the omitted intermediate frames, and the visual sequence composed of transmitted anchors and reconstructed frames is fed into the cloud-side VSLAM estimator.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig2.png}
\caption{Overall framework of VLMem-Codec for Edge--Cloud VSLAM. VLMem denotes Vision--Language Memory, and the transmitted VLMem payload consists of visual anchors and language cues. The cloud-side world-model decoder reconstructs omitted frames from the VLMem payload, while MGDR (Memory-Guided Decoder Refinement) uses the same conditions to refine the decoder for downstream VSLAM.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure*}

Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the overall pipeline of VLMem-Codec. The edge performs lightweight VLMem encoding, selects visual anchors at admissible temporal positions, and constructs language cues. The transmission stage uploads only the compact VLMem payload rather than the full dense image stream, while the cloud recovers task-usable visual continuity from the transmitted payload. The framework contains both an inference branch and an MGDR refinement branch. The inference branch performs world-model reconstruction from the transmitted VLMem payload. The MGDR branch constructs refinement samples using visual anchors and language cues in the same format, so that the decoder receives consistent conditioning forms during refinement and inference.

Let the dense visual sequence captured by the edge device be
\begin{equation}
I_{1:T}=\{I_t\}_{t=1}^{T},\quad
I_t\in \mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times C}.
\label{eq:dense_stream}
\end{equation}
Here, \(I_t\) denotes the \(t\)-th image in the sequence, \(T\) is the sequence length, and \(H\), \(W\), and \(C\) denote image height, width, and channels, respectively. The goal of VLMem-Codec is not to transmit \(I_{1:T}\) with frame-by-frame high fidelity. Instead, it aims to substantially reduce the upload payload while preserving visual structure and temporal continuity sufficient for cloud-side VSLAM.

The edge encoder divides the dense sequence into VLMem units. Let \(P\subseteq\{1,\ldots,T\}\) denote the set of admissible temporal positions for selecting visual anchors and event candidates. The \(i\)-th VLMem unit is defined as
\begin{equation}
U_i=\left(I_{b_i^-}, I_{b_i^+}, M_i, S_i, L_i, L_i^-\right),
\quad b_i^-, b_i^+\in P.
\label{eq:vl_memory_unit}
\end{equation}
Here, \(I_{b_i^-}\) and \(I_{b_i^+}\) are the start and end visual anchors of the unit, providing endpoint appearance and geometric constraints. \(M_i\) denotes a compact motion cue or unit-level motion state, and \(S_i\) denotes the semantic event-anchor set; both are produced by the motion--semantic event segmentation process described later. \(L_i\) and \(L_i^-\) denote positive and negative language cues, respectively, which constrain cloud-side reconstruction. The primary representation of the VLMem payload remains visual anchors and language cues. Compact motion cues, semantic event anchors, and metadata are used mainly to organize VLMem units and construct language cues.

The edge-side memory encoder and cloud-side world-model decoder can be written compactly as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{V}
&=\mathcal{E}_{\phi}(I_{1:T};P)
=\{U_i\}_{i=1}^{N},\\
\hat{I}_{1:T}
&=\mathcal{D}_{\theta+\Delta\theta}(\mathcal{V}).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:memory_coding}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{E}_{\phi}\) denotes the edge-side VLMem encoder, \(\mathcal{V}\) denotes the transmitted VLMem payload, and \(\mathcal{D}_{\theta+\Delta\theta}\) denotes the cloud-side world-model decoder. The reconstructed sequence \(\hat{I}_{1:T}\) is composed of transmitted visual anchors and omitted intermediate frames filled in by the world-model decoder. The parameter \(\theta\) denotes the pretrained world-model parameters, and \(\Delta\theta\) denotes optional MGDR increments. When \(\Delta\theta=0\), the decoder reduces to the original world model without MGDR refinement.

From the perspective of transmission cost, the VLMem payload size is modeled as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
R(\mathcal{V})
&=
R(K)+\sum_{i=1}^{N}R(M_i,S_i,L_i,L_i^-)\\
&\quad+R(\mathrm{meta}),\\
K&=\{I_b\mid b\in B\}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:transmission_cost}
\end{equation}
Here, \(K\) is the set of transmitted visual anchors, and \(R(K)\) is the main image payload contributed by visual-anchor frames. \(R(M_i,S_i,L_i,L_i^-)\) denotes the lightweight payload of compact motion cues, semantic event anchors, and language cues in each VLMem unit. \(R(\mathrm{meta})\) represents necessary metadata such as temporal indices and unit boundaries. This equation describes the composition and transmission cost of the VLMem payload; it does not assume that all components contribute equally to the total payload.

Rather than formulating VLMem construction as an explicitly solved optimization problem, this work adopts a rate--trajectory evaluation criterion to characterize the relationship between communication efficiency and downstream trajectory quality:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{Q}_{\mathrm{RT}}(\mathcal{V})
&=
\left(
R(\mathcal{V}),
\delta_{\mathrm{slam}}\left(
F_{\mathrm{slam}}(\hat{I}_{1:T}),\mathcal{T}
\right)
\right).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:rate_trajectory_design}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{Q}_{\mathrm{RT}}(\mathcal{V})\) denotes the rate--trajectory evaluation result for a given VLMem payload \(\mathcal{V}\). \(R(\mathcal{V})\) measures the transmitted payload size, \(\delta_{\mathrm{slam}}\) denotes the trajectory-discrepancy metric, \(F_{\mathrm{slam}}\) is the cloud-side VSLAM estimator, and \(\mathcal{T}\) is the reference trajectory when available. In the experiments, trajectory discrepancy is evaluated using APE RMSE, together with transmission reduction, to analyze the communication--trajectory tradeoff of the VLMem representation.

This criterion differs from conventional rate--distortion optimization in image or video coding. Traditional coding usually defines distortion as pixel error or perceptual reconstruction error, whereas this work focuses on trajectory discrepancy after the reconstructed sequence is consumed by downstream VSLAM. Importantly, \(\mathcal{Q}_{\mathrm{RT}}\) is an evaluation criterion, not a closed-form optimization objective solved by the encoder. VLMem-Codec constructs \(\mathcal{V}\) through motion--semantic event segmentation, visual-anchor selection, language-cue construction, and cloud-side world-model reconstruction, and evaluates its task usability through payload size and APE RMSE.

\subsection{Motion--Semantic Event Segmentation}
\label{subsec:motion_semantic_segmentation}

The edge encoder constructs event units from two complementary responses: Motion Response and Semantic Response. Motion Response characterizes viewpoint-change trends between adjacent frames and is used to identify motion states such as forward motion, turning, and mixed transitions that affect reconstruction difficulty. Semantic Response captures changes in scene content or semantic structure and is used to detect positions where visual content changes significantly even when the motion state remains stable. Together, the two responses determine event boundaries and further form the visual anchors, unit-level motion cue \(M_i\), and semantic event-anchor set \(S_i\) in each VLMem unit. The goal of this stage is not to estimate an accurate camera trajectory on the edge, but to extract low-cost structured event information for visual-anchor selection and language-cue construction.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/fig3.png}
\caption{Dual-response fusion event segmentation. Motion Response and Semantic Response are fused to identify Event Boundaries and Event Units; this intermediate stage precedes final VLMem payload construction from visual anchors and language cues.}
\label{fig:event_segmentation}
\end{figure*}

Phase correlation is used to generate lightweight Motion Response. Its basic principle is that when two images mainly differ by a translational displacement, the displacement appears as a phase difference in the Fourier domain. Applying the inverse Fourier transform to the normalized cross-power spectrum produces a peak at the corresponding displacement. Therefore, the peak location can be used to estimate the relative displacement of a local image block, while the peak strength provides a confidence cue. Compared with dense optical flow or feature tracking, phase correlation does not require pixel-wise correspondence and is less dependent on stable local features, making it suitable for generating coarse motion-state evidence on the edge.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:event_segmentation}, the encoder divides adjacent frames into spatial blocks and computes phase correlation for each block. Let \(I_t^g\) denote the \(g\)-th image block in frame \(t\), and let \(H\) be a local windowing function. The block-wise phase-correlation response is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
C_{t,g}
&=
\mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\left(
\frac{
\mathcal{F}(H\odot I_t^g)
\mathcal{F}(H\odot I_{t-1}^g)^{\ast}}
{\left|
\mathcal{F}(H\odot I_t^g)
\mathcal{F}(H\odot I_{t-1}^g)^{\ast}
\right|+\epsilon}\right),\\
(d_{t,g},\rho_{t,g})
&=\operatorname{Peak}(C_{t,g}).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:block_phase_correlation}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication, \(\mathcal{F}\) is the Fourier transform, \((\cdot)^\ast\) denotes complex conjugation, \(d_{t,g}\) is the block-level displacement, and \(\rho_{t,g}\) is the peak confidence. To suppress the influence of weakly textured regions and unstable peaks on global motion judgment, the encoder reliability-weights block responses according to peak confidence, local texture strength, and block location, and then aggregates block-level displacements into low-dimensional motion descriptors:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\bar{d}_t
&=
\frac{\sum_g w_{t,g}d_{t,g}}
{\sum_g w_{t,g}+\epsilon},\\
\chi_t
&=
\frac{
\sum_g w_{t,g}\left(x'_g d^x_{t,g}+y'_g d^y_{t,g}\right)}
{\sum_g w_{t,g}\left((x'_g)^2+(y'_g)^2\right)+\epsilon}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:motion_descriptors}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\bar{d}_t\) denotes the reliability-weighted global displacement trend, \(\chi_t\) denotes radial expansion evidence related to forward motion, and \((x'_g,y'_g)\) are normalized block-center coordinates. The encoder then maps these low-dimensional descriptors, together with the frame-level confidence \(\rho_t\), into a coarse motion state such as forward, left-turn, right-turn, or mixed. To avoid over-segmentation caused by single-frame noise, frame-level motion states are stabilized over admissible temporal windows and then aggregated within each event unit to form the unit-level motion cue \(M_i\).

Motion Response mainly reflects viewpoint changes, but it cannot cover all changes in visual content. For example, during long straight motion or slow movement, the overall motion state may remain stable while salient objects appear, disappear, or change semantic context. If event units are constructed only from Motion Response, long stable-motion intervals may become semantically underconstrained. Therefore, the encoder further introduces Semantic Response based on image-embedding changes.

For a candidate admissible position \(p\) and the current semantic context \(c\), the encoder computes normalized visual embeddings and defines Semantic Response by their embedding discrepancy:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
z_p
&=\frac{f_{\psi}(I_p)}{\|f_{\psi}(I_p)\|_2},
\qquad
z_c
=\frac{f_{\psi}(I_c)}{\|f_{\psi}(I_c)\|_2},\\
r^{\mathrm{S}}_p(c)
&=1-z_p^{\top}z_c.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:semantic_response}
\end{equation}
Here, \(f_{\psi}\) is the image embedding encoder, \(z_p\) and \(z_c\) are the normalized embeddings of the candidate position and the current semantic context, respectively, and \(r^{\mathrm{S}}_p(c)\) measures the semantic-change strength of the candidate position relative to the current context. The context \(c\) need not be the immediately preceding frame; it is the active scene/content reference used for judging content changes within the current event unit. To avoid false detections caused by single-frame noise, the encoder filters candidate semantic event anchors using persistence and separation constraints:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}
&=
\left\{
a\in P
\;\middle|\;
\operatorname{Pers}_{P_{\min}}
\left(r^{\mathrm{S}}_a(c)>\tau_a(m)\right),
\right.\\
&\qquad\left.
\operatorname{Sep}(a;c,b^+)\ge g_{\min}
\right\}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:semantic_anchor_admission}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}\) is the candidate semantic event-anchor set. The operator \(\operatorname{Pers}_{P_{\min}}\) requires the semantic change to persist over enough admissible positions, and \(\operatorname{Sep}\) enforces sufficient separation between a candidate semantic anchor, the current context, and the unit boundary. The threshold \(\tau_a(m)\) is adjusted according to the current motion state. These semantic event anchors represent scene/content changes in the embedding space rather than manually labeled object categories.

After obtaining motion states and candidate semantic event anchors, the encoder constructs event boundaries sequentially. Let \(\Delta_m\) be the admissible unit-span set associated with the current motion state \(m\), and let \(e\) denote the endpoint of the current interval to be covered. From the current boundary \(b_k\), the candidate next-boundary set is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{N}_k
&=
\left\{
p\in P
\;\middle|\;
b_k<p\le e,\;
p-b_k\in\Delta_m,\right.\\
&\qquad\left.
\operatorname{Reach}(p,e;\Delta_m)=1
\right\}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:candidate_boundary_set}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{N}_k\) contains all feasible next event-boundary positions, and \(\operatorname{Reach}(p,e;\Delta_m)\) ensures that the remaining interval from \(p\) to \(e\) can still be covered by valid spans. Once the candidate set is determined, the encoder selects the earliest feasible semantic event anchor if such an anchor exists; otherwise, it selects the farthest reachable boundary:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
b_{k+1}
&=\min\left(\mathcal{N}_k\cap\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}\right),
&& \text{if } \mathcal{N}_k\cap\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{sem}}\neq\varnothing,\\
b_{k+1}
&=\max \mathcal{N}_k,
&& \text{otherwise}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:admissible_boundary_construction}
\end{equation}
This procedure generates event boundaries sequentially rather than solving a global optimal segmentation problem. Adjacent event boundaries define an event unit, whose start and end positions correspond to the visual-anchor indices \(b_i^-\) and \(b_i^+\) in the VLMem unit defined in Sec.~III-A. Within the event unit, stabilized motion states are aggregated into the unit-level motion cue \(M_i\), and candidate semantic event anchors falling inside the interval are collected as the semantic event-anchor set \(S_i\). Finally, each event unit, together with its two visual anchors, unit-level motion cue \(M_i\), semantic event-anchor set \(S_i\), and language cues, forms the VLMem unit. In this construction, Motion Response controls the length of intervals with large viewpoint changes, while Semantic Response supplements scene-content constraints, allowing the VLMem payload to preserve reconstruction-relevant structure under low transmission cost.

\subsection{Dual-Branch World-Model Decoding}
\label{subsec:wm_lora_decoding}

The cloud-side decoder contains two related branches: an inference branch and an MGDR refinement branch. The inference branch reconstructs omitted intermediate frames from the transmitted VLMem payload \(\mathcal{V}\) and forms a reconstructed sequence that can be consumed by the cloud-side VSLAM estimator. The MGDR branch constructs refinement samples using visual anchors and language cues in the same format as inference-time inputs, so that the decoder receives consistent conditioning during refinement and deployment. Both branches share the same VLMem conditioning interface, allowing the decoder to recover intermediate frames around visual anchors, motion cues, semantic cues, and reconstruction constraints.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig4.png}
\caption{MGDR mechanism for world-model reconstruction. The inference branch reconstructs intermediate frames from transmitted visual anchors and language cues, while the refinement branch constructs MGDR pairs and learns MGDR increments \(\Delta W\) as LoRA-based low-rank increments with the pretrained world model frozen.}
\label{fig:lora_decoder}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:lora_decoder} illustrates the dual-branch world-model decoding process. For each VLMem unit, visual anchors provide endpoint appearance and geometric constraints that are difficult to express precisely using language alone, while language cues provide compact conditions about motion state, semantic changes, scene context, and reconstruction quality. Given the unit-level motion cue \(M_i\) and semantic event-anchor set \(S_i\) from Sec.~III-B, the language cues for the \(i\)-th unit are constructed as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
L_i
&=
\operatorname{Join}
\left(
L_{\mathrm{base}},
L_{\mathrm{motion}}(M_i),
L_{\mathrm{semantic}}(S_i)
\right),\\
L_i^- &= L_{\mathrm{neg}}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:language_memory_construction}
\end{equation}
Here, \(L_{\mathrm{base}}\) denotes a shared navigation-view prior that maintains the basic first-person visual style and navigation-scene constraints; \(L_{\mathrm{motion}}(M_i)\) injects motion conditions according to the unit-level motion cue, such as forward motion, turning, or mixed motion; \(L_{\mathrm{semantic}}(S_i)\) denotes semantic constraints derived from the semantic event-anchor set, which indicate scene content, salient objects, or contextual changes; and \(L_{\mathrm{neg}}\) is the negative language cue used to suppress blur, distortion, drift, abrupt viewpoint changes, and hallucinated objects that may be harmful to VSLAM reconstruction. When no significant semantic event exists within a unit, \(L_{\mathrm{semantic}}(S_i)\) can degenerate to a stable-scene constraint or an empty semantic constraint. In this way, language cues carry not only unit-level motion state but also content changes detected by Semantic Response.

For the \(i\)-th VLMem unit, the visual anchors are \(I_{b_i^-}\) and \(I_{b_i^+}\). To specify the frames to be filled by the cloud-side decoder, we define the omitted intermediate-frame index set as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{T}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}
&=
\left\{
t\mid b_i^-<t<b_i^+
\right\},\\
I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}
&=
\left\{
I_t
\right\}_{t\in\mathcal{T}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:mid_interval}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{T}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\) denotes the time indices between the two visual anchors that are not directly uploaded, and \(I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\) denotes the corresponding original intermediate frames. This definition provides a unified temporal range for inference-time reconstruction outputs and MGDR supervision targets. In other words, each VLMem unit is decoded by filling the omitted frames inside the unit under known start anchors, end anchors, and language cues.

During inference, the cloud-side decoder first forms the conditioning tuple for the \(i\)-th unit. The tuple includes the two visual anchors, the positive language cue, the negative language cue, and the number of intermediate frames to be filled:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
c_i
&=
\left(
I_{b_i^-},
I_{b_i^+},
L_i,
L_i^-,
n_i
\right),\\
n_i
&=
\left|
\mathcal{T}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}
\right|,\\
\hat{I}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}
&=
\mathcal{D}_{\theta+\Delta\theta}(c_i).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:conditional_reconstruction}
\end{equation}
Here, \(c_i\) is the conditioning input of the world-model decoder, \(n_i\) specifies the number of intermediate frames to reconstruct in the unit, and \(\hat{I}_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\) denotes the reconstructed intermediate-frame set output by the decoder. The two visual anchors constrain the start and end views of the unit, the language cues provide motion-state, semantic-change, and reconstruction-quality constraints, and the completion length \(n_i\) aligns the decoder output with the temporal span of the VLMem unit. The reconstructed sequence consumed by cloud-side VSLAM is formed by retaining transmitted visual anchors and inserting the reconstructed intermediate frames of all units. Adjacent units share boundary anchors, i.e., \(b_i^+=b_{i+1}^-\), which provides consistent visual connections at unit boundaries.

Because the objective of a generic world model for video generation is not necessarily aligned with the geometric continuity and trackable structures required by VSLAM, this work introduces MGDR to adapt the cloud-side decoder under VLMem conditions. The MGDR branch converts original training sequences into refinement samples that have the same conditioning format as inference. Each sample consists of a conditioning tuple and its corresponding target intermediate frames:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{MGDR}}
=
\left\{
\left(c_i,I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\right)
\right\}_{i=1}^{N_d}.
\label{eq:refinement_pairs}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{MGDR}}\) denotes the MGDR refinement-sample set, \(c_i\) has the same format as the conditioning input used during inference, and \(I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\) is the corresponding target intermediate-frame set. This construction exposes the decoder during refinement to the same combination of visual anchors, language cues, and completion length used during deployment, allowing MGDR to focus on intermediate-frame reconstruction under VLMem conditions.

MGDR is implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments while keeping the pretrained world-model parameters frozen. For a selected layer weight \(W_\ell\), the MGDR increment is parameterized as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta W_{\ell}
&=
\frac{\alpha_\ell}{r}
B_\ell A_\ell,\\
A_\ell&\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times d_\ell},
\quad
B_\ell\in\mathbb{R}^{d'_\ell\times r},\\
r&\ll \min(d_\ell,d'_\ell).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:lora_refinement_space}
\end{equation}
Here, \(W_\ell\) is a layer weight in the pretrained world model, \(\Delta W_\ell\) is the low-rank MGDR increment applied to that layer, \(r\) is the low-rank dimension, and \(\alpha_\ell\) is the layer scaling factor. Only \(A_\ell\) and \(B_\ell\) are trained during refinement, while the pretrained world-model parameters \(\theta\) remain frozen. The low-rank increments of all selected layers jointly form \(\Delta\theta=\{\Delta W_\ell\}\). This formulation allows MGDR to adapt to VLMem conditions with a small parameter increment without retraining the entire world model.

Given the MGDR refinement-sample set, the decoder refinement objective is
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Delta\theta^\star
&=
\arg\min_{\Delta\theta\in\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{LoRA}}}
\sum_{\left(c_i,I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}\right)\in\mathcal{P}_{\mathrm{MGDR}}}\\
&\qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gen}}
\left(
\mathcal{D}_{\theta+\Delta\theta}(c_i),
I_i^{\mathrm{mid}}
\right).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:memory_guided_refinement}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{LoRA}}\) denotes the increment parameter space restricted by low-rank updates, and \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gen}}\) is the generation loss of the underlying video world model. The objective trains the decoder to reconstruct intermediate frames from two visual anchors, motion/semantic language cues, and completion length, thereby improving its adaptation to reconstruction under VLMem conditions.

MGDR does not aim for pixel-identical reconstruction. Instead, it biases the decoder under the same VLMem conditions toward visual continuity, viewpoint stability, geometric consistency, and trackable structures useful for VSLAM. Therefore, reconstructed frames are treated as task-usable visual reconstruction for downstream VSLAM, and their value is evaluated mainly through trajectory quality and decoder-ablation experiments.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp_setup}

The evaluation uses three types of visual sequences: NCD, TUM, and GDUT. NCD and TUM are public benchmark datasets, while GDUT contains real-world robot data collected in our deployment environment. This combination allows the experiments to cover both standard datasets and robot-captured visual streams under the same \VLMemCodec{} evaluation protocol. Fig.~\ref{fig:experimental_platform} shows the edge--cloud hardware platform used for the GDUT experiments.

\begin{figure}[!b]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig5.png}
\caption{Edge--cloud experimental platform used in the real-world GDUT experiments. The edge platform consists of an AGV equipped with a RealSense D455 camera, an onboard NUC, and a Livox MID-360 LiDAR, while the cloud platform is a multi-GPU server connected through a low-bandwidth sparse transmission setting.}
\label{fig:experimental_platform}
\end{figure}

The edge platform consists of an AGV equipped with a RealSense D455 camera, an onboard NUC, and a Livox MID-360 LiDAR. The cloud platform is a multi-GPU server connected to the edge through a bandwidth-constrained sparse transmission setting. In this configuration, the edge device encodes the raw visual stream into a VLMem payload, while the cloud performs world-model reconstruction and VSLAM evaluation.

All experiments follow a unified end-to-end evaluation pipeline. The preparation stage organizes image sequences and trajectory references; the encoding stage converts dense frames into the transmitted VLMem payload; the decoding stage generates reconstructed sequences from visual anchors and language cues; and the evaluation stage runs the VSLAM estimator on both original and reconstructed sequences. Unless otherwise stated, phase correlation (PC) is used to generate the edge-side Motion Response. The cloud uses a vision--language conditioned world-model decoder. In the decoder ablation, WM denotes the original world-model decoder, and WM+MGDR denotes the same decoder refined through MGDR.

Trajectory quality is measured from the VSLAM estimator output using evo after Sim(3) alignment, and the main task metric is absolute pose error (APE) RMSE. Compression is measured by comparing the original image-sequence size with the transmitted VLMem payload size. LPIPS, SSIM, and FID are used only as auxiliary image-fidelity metrics in the MGDR ablation. They provide complementary diagnostics of reconstructed image appearance, but are not the final task-level objective of \VLMemCodec{}.

The first experiment evaluates end-to-end transmission reduction and VSLAM trajectory accuracy across nine test fragments.

The main interpretation of Table~\ref{tab:compression_accuracy} is therefore the compression--trajectory tradeoff: the transmitted VLMem payload greatly reduces visual data while REC APE remains close to ORI APE on average. \section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ==================================================

\VLMemCodec{} is designed for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM, rather than generic video delivery or general image enhancement. Its core idea is to represent dense visual streams as a compact VLMem payload composed of visual anchors and language cues, and to recover downstream VSLAM-usable visual continuity using a cloud-side world-model decoder. Therefore, the primary evaluation focus is not frame-by-frame pixel fidelity, but trajectory quality after the reconstructed sequence is consumed by VSLAM. In other words, \VLMemCodec{} provides a task-oriented visual coding and reconstruction scheme whose effectiveness should be interpreted jointly through transmitted payload size, trajectory accuracy, and auxiliary image metrics.

The experimental results also show that image-fidelity metrics and downstream VSLAM trajectory quality are not fully aligned. LPIPS, SSIM, and FID remain useful auxiliary metrics because they characterize perceptual similarity or distribution-level appearance, but they do not necessarily vary consistently with APE RMSE. In the MGDR ablation, decoder refinement implemented through low-rank increments reduces REC APE, while auxiliary image-fidelity metrics do not improve uniformly. This suggests that, for Edge--Cloud VSLAM visual transmission, whether a reconstruction is useful for trajectory estimation depends more on geometric continuity, viewpoint stability, and trackable structures than on pixel-level or perceptual similarity alone.

The current prototype still has runtime limitations. The VLMem payload substantially reduces the amount of data transmitted from the edge to the cloud, but cloud-side world-model reconstruction remains the major time-consuming component in the current implementation. Therefore, the reported results should be interpreted as a validation of the efficient visual transmission formulation and the effectiveness of the VLMem payload, not as a claim of a fully real-time cloud service. Practical end-to-end latency also depends on the scale of the world-model decoder, GPU memory and parallelism, batching strategy, inference acceleration, and parallel reconstruction of VLMem units.

The reconstructed frames are also limited by the world model and the quality of the conditioning signals. Although visual anchors and language cues constrain reconstruction, the reconstructed sequence may still contain local hallucinations, geometric inconsistency, viewpoint drift, dynamic-object artifacts, or texture changes. Therefore, reconstructed frames should be treated as task-usable visual reconstructions for downstream VSLAM evaluation, rather than physically exact observations. The method also depends on the quality of motion--semantic event segmentation, language-cue construction, and world-model reconstruction; errors in any of these stages can reduce the VSLAM usability of the reconstructed sequence. Future work will further study more efficient world-model serving, model compression or distillation, parallel decoding of VLMem units, geometry-aware constraints, uncertainty-aware reconstruction, and lightweight task-specific decoders for Edge--Cloud VSLAM.

% ==================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==================================================

This paper presented \VLMemCodec{}, a Vision--Language Memory coding framework for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM. The framework converts dense image streams into a compact VLMem payload composed of visual anchors and language cues, and reconstructs task-usable visual sequences on the cloud using a vision--language conditioned world-model decoder. Through motion--semantic event segmentation, \VLMemCodec{} selects key visual anchors and constructs motion and semantic cues; through MGDR, the decoder is further adapted under the same VLMem conditions toward the visual continuity, geometric consistency, and trackable structures required by downstream VSLAM.

The experimental results show that the VLMem payload can substantially reduce edge-to-cloud visual transmission while keeping reconstructed-sequence VSLAM trajectory error close to original-sequence results on average. Phase correlation provides low-cost Motion Response for edge-side event encoding, and MGDR reduces REC APE in the decoder ablation. The results also show that auxiliary image-fidelity metrics are not fully aligned with VSLAM trajectory quality, further indicating that Edge--Cloud VSLAM visual transmission should be evaluated around downstream task usability. Future work will further improve reconstruction geometric consistency, cloud-side decoding efficiency, and VSLAM-aware reconstruction constraints.

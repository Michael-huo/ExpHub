\section{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ==================================================

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp_setup}

The evaluation uses three types of sequences: NCD, TUM, and GDUT. NCD and TUM provide public benchmark sequences, while GDUT contains real robot data collected in our deployment environment. This combination allows the experiments to cover both standard datasets and robot-captured visual streams under the same \VLMemCodec{} evaluation protocol. Fig.~\ref{fig:experimental_platform} shows the edge--cloud hardware platform used for the real-world GDUT experiments.

\begin{figure}[!b]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig5.png}
\caption{Edge--cloud experimental platform used in the real-world GDUT experiments. The edge platform consists of an AGV equipped with a RealSense D455 camera, an onboard NUC, and a Livox MID-360 LiDAR, while the cloud platform is a multi-GPU server connected through a low-bandwidth sparse transmission setting.}
\label{fig:experimental_platform}
\end{figure}

The edge platform consists of an AGV equipped with a RealSense D455 camera, an onboard NUC, and a Livox MID-360 LiDAR. The cloud platform is a multi-GPU server connected through a sparse transmission setting. In this configuration, the edge device encodes a VLMem payload from raw visual streams, and the cloud performs world-model reconstruction and VSLAM evaluation.

All experiments follow a unified end-to-end evaluation pipeline. The preparation stage organizes image sequences and trajectory references; the encoder converts dense frames into the transmitted VLMem payload; the decoder produces a reconstructed sequence from visual anchors and language cues; and the evaluation stage runs the VSLAM estimator on both original and reconstructed sequences. Unless otherwise stated, phase correlation (PC) is used as the edge-side motion estimator. The cloud uses a vision--language conditioned world-model decoder. We compare the original world-model decoder (WM decoder) with the same decoder refined through MGDR (WM+MGDR decoder). In the ablation, WM denotes the original world-model decoder, while WM+MGDR denotes the world-model decoder refined through MGDR, implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments.

Trajectory quality is measured from the VSLAM estimator output using evo after Sim(3) alignment, and the reported task metric is APE RMSE. Compression is measured by comparing the size of the original image sequence with the size of the transmitted VLMem payload. LPIPS, SSIM, and FID are used only as auxiliary image-fidelity metrics in the MGDR ablation; they are not the final task-level objective of \VLMemCodec.

\subsection{End-to-End Compression and VSLAM Accuracy}
\label{subsec:compression_accuracy}

The first experiment evaluates end-to-end transmission reduction and VSLAM trajectory accuracy across nine test fragments.

Table~\ref{tab:compression_accuracy} reports that, on average, the raw image sequence size is 403.42 MiB, while the transmitted VLMem payload is 11.06 MiB. This corresponds to an average data reduction of 96.8\%, indicating that the proposed representation substantially reduces transmitted visual data.

The VSLAM results indicate that this reduction can retain task-usable geometric information. ORI and REC denote the original and reconstructed sequences, respectively. Table~\ref{tab:compression_accuracy} still reports absolute APE RMSE, but it now also lists the GT path length \(L_{\mathrm{GT}}\) so each RMSE value can be read with its trajectory scale. The path-normalized deviation \(|\Delta|/L_{\mathrm{GT}}\) helps avoid over-interpreting large absolute RMSE changes on longer trajectories or percentage changes when ORI APE is very small.

The average ORI APE is 0.698 m, while the average REC APE is 0.735 m. \(\Delta\)APE is computed as REC APE minus ORI APE, giving an average signed difference of +0.037 m and an average path-normalized deviation of 0.898\%. These averages should be interpreted together with the sequence-level variation. Different fragments contain different scene texture, motion patterns, reconstruction difficulty, and VSLAM estimator sensitivity, so the same memory-based transmission mechanism can affect feature availability and temporal continuity differently across sequences. Some reconstructed sequences obtain lower APE than their original counterparts, whereas others obtain higher APE. A negative \(\Delta\)APE is not a physical-quality claim about reconstructed frames; it only indicates that the VSLAM estimator estimated a lower aligned trajectory error on that reconstructed sequence. This behavior is expected because \VLMemCodec{} does not aim to reconstruct pixel-identical videos, but to retain enough geometric continuity and trackable texture for downstream VSLAM.

The main interpretation of Table~\ref{tab:compression_accuracy} is therefore the compression--trajectory tradeoff: the transmitted VLMem payload greatly reduces visual data while REC APE remains close to ORI APE on average. This supports the use of Vision--Language Memory for task-usable visual reconstruction under the reported evaluation protocol, rather than claiming frame-level physical fidelity.

Fig.~\ref{fig:trajectory_map} complements the quantitative results in Table~\ref{tab:compression_accuracy} with representative visual evidence. The global trajectory overlays show that the REC trajectories preserve the main route structures relative to ORI and GT under high transmission reduction. To further examine regions where global plots alone are less informative, Fig.~\ref{fig:trajectory_map} highlights representative local turning segments and shows the corresponding start frames, reconstructed intermediate frames, and end frames. These examples indicate that world-model reconstruction can provide temporally continuous views around local motion changes, helping maintain task-usable visual structure for downstream VSLAM. This qualitative evidence supports the trajectory-preservation interpretation of VLMem-Codec, without implying pixel-identical or physically exact frame recovery.

\subsection{Payload Size and Runtime Breakdown}
\label{subsec:payload_runtime}

To further analyze transmission behavior, we evaluate representative 10 s, 20 s, and 40 s GDUT clips at 24 FPS. The clips contain 241, 481, and 961 frames, cover 7.19 m, 14.48 m, and 28.97 m GT paths, and transmit 5, 8, and 13 visual anchors, respectively. Table~\ref{tab:payload_runtime} reports the clip-level payload and encoding-time comparison among VLMem, fixed 10 Mbps H.265, and Raw ZIP. Fig.~\ref{fig:exp2_payload_runtime}(a)(b) visualizes the same horizontal comparison, while Fig.~\ref{fig:exp2_payload_runtime}(c) separately summarizes the VLMem-Codec internal runtime composition, including VLMem encoding and cloud-side decoding.

The results show that VLMem payload growth is slower than clip-duration growth: payloads increase from 1.98 MiB to 5.10 MiB, while the advantage over the fixed 10 Mbps H.265 reference increases from 6.0$\times$ to 9.2$\times$. VLMem encoding is lower than H.265 encoding for the 20 s and 40 s clips. H.265 is included only as a payload-size and encoding-time reference, not as a downstream VSLAM accuracy baseline, and no real-time deployment claim is made from these prototype timings.

\subsection{Edge-Side Motion Estimation Benchmark}
\label{subsec:motion_benchmark}

The third experiment compares three edge-side motion estimation choices for VLMem event encoding. The encoder requires coarse but reliable motion-trend evidence for event segmentation and motion-conditioned language cues. It does not require accurate camera-trajectory recovery, dense pixelwise correspondence, or long-term feature tracks, so the comparison focuses on lightweight global motion evidence rather than precise trajectory estimation.

Table~\ref{tab:motion_benchmark} summarizes the benchmark on the 40 s GDUT clip.

PC is used here as a motion-response generator, not as a precise trajectory estimator. It is the lightest option, requiring 4.51 s in total and 4.70 ms per frame pair. ORB feature tracking requires 11.73 s, or 2.60$\times$ the PC cost, and produces usable motion evidence for 85.7\% of the evaluated pairs. Farneback optical flow (OF) provides a dense motion field, but its runtime increases to 29.75 s, or 6.59$\times$ the PC cost.

This comparison reflects the abstraction level required by the encoder. The motion response does not need pixelwise correspondence or dense local motion; it needs low-cost evidence for coarse motion states and event boundaries that can be converted into language cues. ORB is a useful comparison because it is closer to VSLAM front-end feature-tracking evidence, but rapid viewpoint changes, sparse features, blur, or low texture can make its valid response unreliable. OF is useful as a dense-motion reference, but its edge-side computational cost is higher than needed for VLMem event encoding. PC therefore provides the most suitable trade-off in this benchmark for this paper's abstraction level: coarse motion trend, low cost, and robust valid response.

Table~\ref{tab:motion_benchmark} quantifies the computational tradeoff among PC, ORB feature tracking, and optical flow, while Fig.~\ref{fig:motion_benchmark} illustrates how the selected PC-based response is used inside VLMem encoding. The PC response provides lightweight coarse motion-event evidence rather than accurate camera-trajectory estimation. In Fig.~\ref{fig:motion_benchmark}(a), the numbered response peaks correspond to selected motion events that can be localized along the trajectory-map reference in Fig.~\ref{fig:motion_benchmark}(b), supporting the use of PC as a coarse motion-state segmentation signal. The lower part of Fig.~\ref{fig:motion_benchmark} further examines event~3 through a motion-aware language-cue ablation. REC-A denotes a reconstructed sequence without motion-aware cues, whereas REC-B denotes a reconstructed sequence with motion-aware cues. REC-A shows less stable temporal viewpoint evolution, whereas REC-B preserves a more motion-consistent visual transition during the selected motion event. This result indicates that the motion response is not used as an end in itself, but as a lightweight cue source for constructing motion-aware cues that improve task-usable world-model reconstruction.

\subsection{MGDR Ablation for the World-Model Decoder}
\label{subsec:lora_ablation}

The final experiment ablates whether MGDR refines the world-model decoder toward VSLAM-useful reconstructed frames under the same VLMem input. It is not a general claim about perceptual image quality. MGDR is implemented through low-rank increments, while the visible ablation compares WM and WM+MGDR.

\input{tables/tab_exp3_lora_ablation}

Table~\ref{tab:lora_ablation} reports APE values as RMSE in meters, while LPIPS, SSIM, and FID are auxiliary image-fidelity metrics. With WM+MGDR, REC APE is reduced from 0.539 m to 0.289 m on NCD, corresponding to a REC APE reduction of 0.250 m; from 0.320 m to 0.224 m on TUM, a reduction of 0.096 m; and from 0.968 m to 0.451 m on GDUT, a reduction of 0.517 m. On average, REC APE is reduced from 0.609 m with WM to 0.321 m with WM+MGDR, a reduction of 0.288 m. ORI APE is unchanged because it is measured on the original sequences and is independent of decoder refinement.

The auxiliary image-fidelity metrics do not follow the same trend as REC APE. On average, LPIPS changes from 0.335 to 0.398, SSIM changes from 0.604 to 0.515, and FID changes from 61.286 to 66.552. LPIPS, SSIM, and FID remain useful auxiliary metrics because they characterize perceptual or distribution-level image appearance, but they measure different properties from downstream VSLAM trajectory quality.

Instead, the ablation indicates that fidelity at the pixel or distribution level is not fully aligned with VSLAM-useful reconstruction in this setting. A reconstruction with weaker pixel-level similarity may be more compatible with VSLAM estimation if it better preserves geometric continuity, viewpoint stability, and trackable structures. MGDR should therefore be interpreted as task-oriented decoder refinement for VSLAM-useful reconstruction, rather than as a general perceptual enhancement module.

Fig.~\ref{fig:lora_qualitative} provides representative qualitative evidence for this mismatch. In the shown transitional views, Fig.~\ref{fig:lora_qualitative} qualitatively suggests that WM+MGDR reduces VSLAM-harmful local geometric distortion, implausible reconstructed structures, and hallucinated content compared with WM. The red-circled regions indicate representative WM failures that may damage feature tracking and geometric continuity, which is more consistent with the observed reduction in downstream REC APE than with conventional image-fidelity metrics alone.

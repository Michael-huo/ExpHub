\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ==================================================

\IEEEPARstart{V}{isual} simultaneous localization and mapping (VSLAM) enables mobile robots, autonomous platforms, and embodied intelligent systems to estimate motion and build maps from visual observations. With the increasing availability of cloud-side computation, edge--cloud VSLAM has become an attractive paradigm: the edge device captures visual observations with limited onboard resources, while the cloud executes more powerful perception, optimization, and mapping modules. This division, however, shifts a critical burden to the communication link. A monocular or RGB-D camera continuously produces high-frame-rate image streams, and transmitting dense visual data from the edge to the cloud incurs substantial bandwidth, energy, and latency costs. The bottleneck becomes more severe in long-term navigation, large-scale deployment, and bandwidth-constrained wireless environments.

Existing image and video compression standards reduce this burden by exploiting spatial and temporal pixel redundancy. Such codecs are highly effective for human viewing, and their rate--distortion objectives are mainly designed around visual fidelity, perceptual quality, and compact delivery of visually faithful frames. These objectives are not explicitly VSLAM-oriented. A compressed stream may spend bits preserving fine appearance details, color variations, or human-perceptual textures that do not necessarily provide stable trackable structures for camera pose estimation.

The mismatch becomes more important when higher compression ratios are pursued. Distortions such as blur, blocking artifacts, texture loss, or temporal inconsistency may weaken feature repeatability, disturb trackable structures, and reduce the geometric cues used by downstream pose estimation. Conversely, VSLAM does not require pixel-identical or high-definition reconstruction of every frame; it requires sufficient geometric consistency, feature repeatability, trackable structures, and scene-motion continuity. Therefore, edge--cloud VSLAM transmission should not be treated merely as conventional video delivery, but as a communication-constrained visual transmission problem in which the transmitted representation is formulated and evaluated by downstream VSLAM trajectory quality. Fig.~\ref{fig:concept} conceptually illustrates this bandwidth bottleneck and the corresponding memory-based motivation, while the quantitative compression and trajectory tradeoffs are reported in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

As a design analogy, this paper is motivated by a human-inspired view of visual memory. When navigating through an environment, humans need not retain every pixel of every frame; they often recall sparse key scenes, landmarks, motion transitions, and high-level semantic impressions. This analogy does not provide a biological model, but it suggests a compact representation for Edge--Cloud VSLAM: a dense visual stream can be represented by VLMem, or Vision--Language Memory. In this representation, visual anchors are boundary visual anchors that preserve the start and end views, appearance, and geometry of a memory unit, while language cues provide compact language-side conditions about motion, scene context, and reconstruction quality. The cloud then uses world-model reconstruction to infer task-usable visual continuity across omitted intervals.

The key intuition is that many pixel-level details can often be omitted when task-useful visual structures are preserved. \VLMemCodec{} is designed to discard more VSLAM-irrelevant visual redundancy while preserving such structures through a VLMem payload that combines visual anchors and language cues. This differs from simple keyframe transmission because the omitted intervals are reconstructed by the world-model decoder under vision--language conditions, rather than being left as gaps between transmitted frames.

Based on this formulation, we propose \emph{\VLMemCodec}, a Vision--Language Memory coding framework with world-model reconstruction for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM. For each VLMem unit, the edge transmits visual anchors as geometric and appearance conditions and encodes language cues that describe motion intent, scene priors, and reconstruction constraints. The visual anchors constrain the beginning and ending views of the unit, while the language cues represent part of the omitted visual information with a compact abstraction. The cloud-side vision--language conditioned world-model decoder uses its learned visual prior to reconstruct task-usable intermediate frames, and the reconstructed sequence is then fed into a cloud-side VSLAM estimator for trajectory estimation.

The key mechanism behind \VLMemCodec{} is the combination of three complementary information sources. First, visual anchors preserve endpoint visual and geometric conditions that are difficult to describe purely by language. Second, language cues encode high-level motion and semantic priors with low communication cost. Third, the cloud-side world-model decoder supplies plausible intermediate visual continuity from its learned prior under the VLMem conditions. In this way, dense visual transmission is reformulated as compact VLMem payload transmission rather than a generic video compression problem.

To make this framework practical for Edge--Cloud VSLAM, \VLMemCodec{} adopts a lightweight edge-side memory encoder. Instead of running heavy online captioning or dense optical-flow networks on the edge, the encoder uses low-cost visual measurements to identify visual anchors and motion-sensitive event structure. These measurements are converted into structured language cues through a language vocabulary and motion-conditioned templates. In the cloud decoding stage, Memory-Guided Decoder Refinement (MGDR) biases the decoder toward navigation-style visual transitions and downstream VSLAM trajectory quality. MGDR is implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments, so the pretrained world model can remain frozen while the decoder is adapted to VLMem conditions. In this work, reconstruction is evaluated primarily by downstream VSLAM trajectory quality using absolute pose error (APE) RMSE, while transmission reduction, edge-side motion-estimation cost, and auxiliary image-fidelity metrics are reported to characterize the tradeoffs.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose \VLMemCodec, a Vision--Language Memory coding framework for efficient visual transmission in Edge--Cloud VSLAM that replaces dense-frame transmission with a compact VLMem payload.
    \item We design a lightweight edge-side VLMem encoder that selects visual anchors, extracts coarse motion and semantic event cues, and converts them into compact language cues without relying on heavy online captioning.
    \item We develop cloud-side world-model reconstruction in which visual anchors and language cues jointly constrain task-usable intermediate-frame reconstruction from omitted visual intervals.
    \item We introduce MGDR, implemented with LoRA-based low-rank increments, to refine the decoder under VLMem conditions without retraining the whole world model, and evaluate WM/WM+MGDR behavior with auxiliary LPIPS, SSIM, and FID metrics.
\end{itemize}
